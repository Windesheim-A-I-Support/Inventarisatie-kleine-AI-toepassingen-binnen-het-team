## 1. What the Bike Library is

The Bike Library is not a collection of bikes.

It is a relationship architecture for everyday mobility — a platform for keeping bicycles in relationship with people across time.

The category is **joint-custodian library everyday mobility**. The first principles are comfort, maintenance, and everyday usefulness. Electric assistance is an enabling layer within that platform, not the category itself.

The platform is not organised around one-time product sale. It is organised around a coherent relationship between rider need, design, service, maintenance, continued circulation, and eventual reincarnation of material.

---

## 2. Origin and lived grounding

The concept has developed from over ten years of direct experience, including:

- Operating a Bike Library experiment in New Zealand for seven years under compromised conditions (wholesale bikes designed and specced to a price) — and learning that the library model itself works even when the hardware is compromised. The NZ operation reached 69% net margin by year three, with hardware never designed for the model. That margin is meaningful evidence: it suggests that when design, manufacturing, distribution, and maintenance are also brought inside the platform, the rolled-up margins can be redirected into lower monthly access for joint custodians.
- Living with seven different cargo bikes across years of daily family use with five children
- Experiencing firsthand what works and fails in real everyday mobility at assisted speeds
- Building and testing service models, care architecture, and custodianship structures in practice

This is not an idea imagined from a market segment. It is a pattern discovered through practice, now being made explicit.

## 3. The category gap

Most e-bike companies still sell bicycles as individual products under product logic.

Some, like Swapfiets, offer a service — but they scale by minimising care per bike. Their model optimises for low-cost access and volume throughput. It cannot move upmarket without breaking what it is.

The e-MTB industry, meanwhile, is built on a different distortion. The industry-facing question is no longer "how fast can a skilled rider descend rough terrain" — it is "how fast can we make the rider think they will descend?" Need is farmed to satisfy invested capital. Each season's limited-edition, race-proven, championship-winning component is sold as the missing piece. That is not the Bike Library's posture. The Bike Library starts from lived need.

The unexplored space is:
**highest quality, highest care, most maintainable, most honest — designed for the rider's body and daily life, not for desire creation or product-cycle pressure.**

Electric bikes changed the speed of everyday cycling. Most bikes did not change enough to protect the rider at that speed. Where people once rode at 14–18 km/h, e-bikes keep ordinary riders close to 25 km/h most of the time. At 14–18 km/h, bad surfaces arrive slowly. At 25 km/h, the same surface becomes a repeated impact field — and the rider's hands, shoulders, neck, jaw, eyes, and brain absorb what the bike fails to absorb.

This is not only a comfort problem. It is a body-protection problem — and specifically, at speed, it becomes a head-stability problem. The deeper framing:

> **EasyUp protects the user's back during loading. The Bike Library platform protects the rider's brain during travel.**

Comfort is not luxury. Comfort is the pre-condition for the bike doing its job. A bike that is unpleasant to ride does not get ridden. An e-bike that does not get ridden is failed car-replacement infrastructure. The entire sustainability argument for urban e-bikes collapses at the comfort threshold.

The reference category for the Bike Library is therefore not other e-bikes. It is the larger set of things people use instead of a bike when daily mobility is uncomfortable: second cars, lease bikes nobody loves, bikeshare fleets nobody cares for, elderly mobility scooters, family school-run car trips, campervan adventure runabouts, and (eventually) small-format delivery vehicles like the future RTS glass-container loop.

## 4. Design principles

### 4.1 Comfort must become invisible

The rider should not need to understand suspension. They should simply experience the result: a ride that feels like a magic carpet. Suspension must reduce shock, fatigue, head shake, and body strain without asking the rider to think about sag, rebound, tokens, compression, or setup complexity.

People should not have to become bike experts to benefit from a better bike.

### 4.2 Design for assisted-era bodies

Traditional bicycle geometry and components were shaped around human power alone. Electric assistance changes the requirements. The goal is no longer maximum human leverage — it is rhythm, clearance, stability, comfort, and easy spin-up.

Shorter cranks (possibly 145–155 mm depending on rider size) may be worth exploring. The crank is no longer only a lever for power. It is an interface for rhythm, comfort, and clearance.

This is a live design question, not a settled prescription.

### 4.3 Full suspension as daily body protection

For this platform, full suspension is not about performance theatre. It is about daily body protection.

At 25 km/h, suspension does not need to be heroic. It needs to be quiet, supportive, predictable, durable, and always working.

Starting direction: 100 mm front travel, 80–100 mm rear travel, coil spring where possible, a small number of spring rates, tuned for seated comfort and load stability — not race performance.

Coil rather than air is a deliberate choice. Air suspension sells adjustability and weight saving. Coil gives repeatability, small-bump comfort, fewer user variables, and less fiddling. For a fleet where consistency matters more than featherweight numbers, fewer variables are mercy.

### 4.4 The frame is a temporary arrangement of material in service of relationship

The frame is not treated as a final object. It is a current form: useful now, maintainable through life, repairable where possible, reusable through pre-loved circulation, and eventually reincarnated back into high-quality raw material.

This points toward:
- A machined alloy structural heart
- Replaceable bolt-on modules
- Fewer welded critical alignment areas
- Easier inspection and replacement
- Future distributed manufacturing potential
- Material reincarnation by design

Precision is not vanity. Precision is care. Misaligned suspension creates waste for the whole life of the bike.

### 4.5 The library carries the care

The rider should not be expected to fix the bike. The platform is supported by mobile service people using cargo bikes as field-service vehicles. The service person comes to the rider. Parts are swapped as modules. The rider is not left alone with mechanical complexity.

Service becomes visible, calm, repeatable, and respectful.

The design goal is to respect the time of both rider and mechanic. That means:
- Eliminate maintenance where possible
- Minimise it where elimination is impossible
- Simplify what remains

## 5. Platform architecture

### 5.1 Central structural node

The heart of the bike is a machined alloy node carrying:

- Motor-gearbox unit
- Main suspension datums
- Battery interface
- Front, rear, and seat/person module mounts
- Service identity and future digital identity / tag

Precision at the heart matters because suspension axis alignment determines bearing life, noise, reliability, and service cost across the full platform life.

### 5.2 Motor-gearbox unit

A sealed motor-gearbox unit is the right answer for this platform. It supports fewer exposed drivetrain parts, no derailleur vulnerability, reduced maintenance variation, easier fleet standardisation, and more predictable service stock.

The platform does not need endless gears. It needs calm, useful range.

The Intradrive GD8 is the current candidate. Twelve years of waiting for a real MGU option for everyday assisted mobility — Pinion was approached two years ago and did not respond (apparently too small a partner to be of interest). Intradrive is now actively open to bike designers and manufacturers building platforms around the GD8 system. The Bike Library is signed up as a designer/manufacturer on that basis.

The GD8 is targeted at e-MTB by Intradrive's primary marketing, but its actual capability — sealed, belt or chain compatible, electronically shifted, EP8-pattern mounted — is at least as well-suited to a comfort-first daily platform. The Bike Library use case may, in fact, help Intradrive prove that the GD8 is shared mobility infrastructure rather than only an e-MTB component.

### 5.3 Modular sections

Rather than many unrelated models:
- Central node remains common
- Rear, front, and seat/person modules change for rider size and use case
- Future cargo/family modules extend the same platform logic

More parts can be acceptable if the system is designed for scale, reuse, pre-loved circulation, and reincarnation.

### 5.4 Seat and access strategy

A suspended seat module may allow the saddle to lower almost out of the way for mounting and dismounting — allowing a stronger frame architecture while improving access. The suspended seat module does three jobs: comfort while riding, fit adjustment, and access when lowered.

When the saddle drops low enough, observation across four years of rental fleet operation shows that most riders naturally swing a leg over the rear wheel rather than thread through the middle of the frame. This means the structural triangle does not need to be sacrificed to assumed step-through need. The frame can stay strong; the rider mounts over the rear.

### 5.5 Front-end service logic

A single-sided or Hossack-style front end is being explored not for novelty but for service speed. If the front wheel can be removed (or the tyre changed) without fighting the frame, brakes, axle, mudguard, and cargo mount each time, then the design has earned its complexity. Punctures and tyre service multiplied across a fleet make every saved minute repeat.

The same logic applies at the rear. The dream service state: belt or chain tension is not a drama, brake caliper does not need fiddling, mudguard does not block removal, and the wheel module can swap quickly. For inspiration: Alan Millyard's mechanical work consistently follows the principle of solving the actual mechanical problem rather than the market's styling problem.

### 5.6 Relationship points

The machine circulates.
The relationship points stay with the person.

Rider keeps: saddle, grips, pedals, possibly handlebar, mirror, bell, phone mount.

Library keeps: frame, motor, gearbox, wheels, brakes, suspension, battery, lighting, service record, reincarnation pathway.

This gives the rider intimacy, hygiene, preference, dignity, and belonging without requiring private ownership of the whole machine.

## 6. Service and maintenance model

The service model works more like fleet aviation than conventional bike retail.

The reference is Southwest Airlines: one aircraft type (the Boeing 737) underpinning the entire operation, which simplifies training, maintenance, scheduling, parts, and crew flexibility. Any staff member can work any flight because the platform is consistent. The Bike Library aims at the same operational logic: one platform, one brake family, one pad, one oil, one tyre family, one wheel interface, one drive unit, one diagnostic path, one reincarnation grammar.

Not endless component variation. Intelligent standardisation:
- One brake family, one pad type, one fluid/oil type
- One tyre family, limited wheel size families
- Standardised bearings and fasteners where possible
- Modular assemblies: wheels, brakes, batteries, suspension units, lighting harnesses

A brake problem is not a custom repair drama. It is a module swap. The repair happens on the bench later.

Downtime is minimised. Service quality is consistent. Mechanic time is respected.

## 7. Wheel sizing

The platform may match wheel size honestly to rider height rather than forcing one standard:
- 24 or 26 inch for smaller riders
- 27.5 inch as the main comfort/utility size
- 29 inch for taller riders or smoother rollover
- 32 inch only if rider truth demands it later

Wheel sizes are added when rider truth demands them, not for product-line complexity.

## 8. Business model

### 8.1 What it is not

Not one-time product sale.
Not extraction from a customer relationship.
Not CLV — customer lifetime value — which assumes the business and its insiders are separate from customers and outsiders, and treats manufacturing, distribution, care, and the rest of the chain as separate units linked by transactions. That framing is structurally incomplete for a coherent custodial model.

### 8.2 What it is

Customer lifetime care = maximum relationship quotient = MOQ 1.

**CLC** replaces CLV as the central measure. The question is not how much value can be extracted from a customer over time. The question is how well the platform keeps a bicycle useful, cared for, and in relationship with people across many lives.

**MRQ = 1**: The system always maintains a direct relationship with each individual. Every person has equal standing. No one is abstracted into a segment or a data point.

**MOQ = 1**: Minimum order quantity equals 1 becomes possible when individual needs are first seen clearly, then collated and coordinated in their fulfillment. The individual need is the true unit. Coordination scales from there, without losing fidelity to the person.

### 8.3 Fair price and honest economics

Fair price cannot be set correctly in advance. Real cost, real use, and real care only reveal themselves through practice, not spreadsheets.

The honest proposal is not a precise price. It is a designed learning system:
- Shared visibility into the full cost stack: design, manufacturing, service, maintenance, replacement, reincarnation, financing, overhead
- Recurring revenue at regular intervals with an agreed margin band
- Retrospective pricing: looking back over a period (12–18 months) and adjusting the price corridor for future cohorts based on real data

The starting evidence is not nothing. The NZ Bike Library reached 69% net margin in year three with hardware never designed for the model. That figure was achieved through care-quality alone, against a backdrop of constant compromise. When the same care logic is paired with design, manufacturing, distribution, and maintenance held inside the platform, the rolled-up margins become available to lower the monthly access cost.

The aim is not to maximise extraction per bike. The aim is to keep the platform useful across many lives.

### 8.4 Disintermediation

The platform collapses fragmented layers — design, manufacturing, wholesale, distribution, retail, maintenance, pre-loved resale, replacement, warranty, service logistics — into one coherent operating relationship.

Unnecessary margins and waste are converted into lower monthly access costs and better service. The system does not depend on a shop dependent on a wholesaler dependent on a brand dependent on a market analyst guessing at need from a distance. It begins from lived need.

## 9. Stage-one strategy

Stage one does not necessarily require a fully new purpose-built platform.

A pragmatic first step may be to reorder existing market solutions intelligently around comfort, maintenance, service visibility, and recurring custodianship — using what is already available, combined with the right service logic and library model.

This:
- Lowers entry friction
- Generates real-world learning (service burden, comfort impact, rider uptake, waste patterns)
- Allows evidence to accumulate before deciding whether and where deeper platform investment is justified

The open question is not whether a perfect answer can be declared in advance. It is where the tipping point lies between early uptake, better everyday comfort, lower service burden, and lower lifetime waste. That balance can only be found by running the experiment.

## 10. Future extensions

### Cargo and family

The same platform logic supports a family/cargo version. Cargo carried in front of the bike and in front of the rider: load visible, central, and emotionally present. Improved ride dynamics by centralising loaded centre of gravity.

Use cases: children, groceries, school bags, everyday errands, older riders with gear, future RTS delivery and glass container pickup.

### EasyUp integration

EasyUp protects the user's back during loading. The Bike Library platform protects the rider's brain during travel. The two pieces of work are siblings in the same design lineage. The EasyUp interface may also support mobile service — allowing bikes to be lifted, held, or moved as part of a cargo-bike-based field-service system.

The load/service interface remains private until patent strategy is secured.

### Return to Steward (RTS)

A later cargo version could serve RTS directly — delivering and collecting glass containers through calm, reliable local loops. First proof remains the comfort bike.

## 12. Open design questions

- Where does the balance lie between stage-one pragmatism (reordered existing solutions) and stage-two ambition (purpose-built platform)? This cannot be declared — only discovered through operation.
- What is the tipping point for comfort adoption: how much comfort improvement drives sustained uptake at scale?
- Does wheel size variation (24–29 inch) need to be addressed from day one, or can a single size serve the first cohort?
- Whether the equation CLC = MRQ = MOQ = 1 appears explicitly in partner-facing documents, or operates behind the prose.
- How to translate the NZ Bike Library 69% net margin into a forward-looking unit-economics sketch that holds up under partner scrutiny.
